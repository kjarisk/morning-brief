The chief executives of seven major European technology companies — ASML, Airbus, Ericsson, Mistral AI, Nokia, SAP, and Siemens — published a joint letter calling on the European Commission to reduce and simplify the EU AI Act's compliance requirements. The letter highlights specific pain points: duplicated documentation obligations that overlap with GDPR requirements, a high-risk classification process that generates bureaucratic friction without proportionate safety benefit, and legal uncertainty that complicates customer contracts.

Mistral AI's inclusion is notable given that the French startup has publicly described regulatory barriers as a competitive disadvantage against US and Chinese peers that operate in less constrained environments. The broader coalition of industrial CEOs signals that the compliance burden is not just a startup concern — established industrial companies with large AI deployments are also feeling the friction.

The timing is significant: the European Commission is scheduled to present its Tech Sovereignty Package on May 27, which is expected to address chip industry support and AI infrastructure investment alongside potential AI Act reforms. Compromise positions circulating in Brussels include simplified high-risk classification, safe harbors for foundation-model training, and reduced duplication between the AI Act and GDPR. The seven companies are effectively lobbying ahead of that package.
