On May 1, 2026, the Pentagon awarded classified-network AI contracts to seven companies: OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon Web Services, Nvidia, SpaceX, and a startup called Reflection AI. Anthropic was the notable absence — the company refused to permit Pentagon use of Claude under "all lawful purposes" language, arguing the phrase could enable domestic mass surveillance or fully autonomous weapons systems.

This is a significant divergence from the rest of the industry. All other major AI labs accepted the Defense Department's standard authorization language. Anthropic's refusal signals that the company is willing to forgo substantial government revenue to maintain its stated policy of not enabling autonomous lethal systems — a position that may cost it considerably as federal AI spending scales up.

The decision has immediate commercial consequences: the classified AI contracts are expected to be worth hundreds of millions of dollars. Whether Anthropic's stance proves principled or commercially costly — or both — will likely become clearer as the Pentagon builds out its AI infrastructure over the next 18 months.
