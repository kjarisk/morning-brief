The step drill is one of the most effective practice tools for fixing the arms-first downswing that causes casting, loss of lag, and distance leaks. The premise is simple: as you start your downswing, physically step your lead foot toward the target before you swing through. The external constraint of the step forces your lower body to initiate the movement — exactly the sequence used by baseball pitchers and every high-speed rotational athlete.

The Rotary Swing approach pairs the step drill with a stomp drill: once the step motion feels natural, you progress to stomping your lead heel as the downswing begins rather than actually stepping. This bridges the gap from the exaggerated drill to a normal-looking full swing, while preserving the lower-body-lead feeling that generates clubhead speed.

The key is to start small: hit 10 balls stepping, then 10 balls making the same internal move without the step. Do this each range session for two to three weeks and the sequencing change becomes automatic. Players who fix early casting with this drill typically gain 10–15 yards of carry without swinging harder, because they are converting wasted lateral motion into stored lag that releases through impact.
